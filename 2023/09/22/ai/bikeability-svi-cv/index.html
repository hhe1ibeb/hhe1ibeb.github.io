Assessing Bikeability with SVI and CV - A Summarization of the Study

Created2023-09-22|Updated2023-09-22
|Post View:

Notes on Ito, K., & Biljecki, F. (2021). Assessing bikeability with street view imagery and computer vision. Transportation Research Part C: Emerging Technologies, 132, 103371. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.trc.2021.103371

Introduction

The questions that the study aims to answer

  1. Can we use CV techniques and SVI data to comprehensively assess bikeability within and among cities?
  2. Can SVI and CV alone be used to assess bikeability in place of traditional techniques?

What is done in this study

  • Develop a bikeability index with 34 indicators under five categories
  • Implement it in Singapore and Tokyo

Contributions

  • Construction of a new comprehensive bikeability index regarding and unprecedented amount of aspects
  • New data collection method that can extract subjective and objective indicators from street-level information
    • at a larger spatial scale
    • across multiple geographies

      We believe that the method and the index may be scaled around the world, including additional cities in future work

Note: the method also includes a survey with a large number of human participants to investigate whether CV techniques may estimate human perception of cycling appeal automatically

Methodology

Preparation

  1. Select study areas

    In this study, Singapore and Tokyo were selected

  2. Obtain street network from OSM to generate points
  3. Randomly select 8000 points for each study area
  4. Collect panorama ID for each sample point

Data Collection

  1. Randomly select 7142 points for each study area

    The number is 7142 because this is the maximum number of SVI images

  2. Retrieve 4 images for each sample point to create panorama images

    We should fix our code

  3. Randomly select 2000 points for perception prediction modelling for each study area
  4. Extract high and low level features for perception
  5. Conduct a survey on randomly selected 4000 images

Composite Bikeability Index Development

Results

  1. SVI indicators had more influence on infrastructure and vehicle-cyclist interaction and also have a stronger correlation with the index with both indicator types.

    $R^2$ of 0.85

  2. Non-SVI indicators like slope and air quality are unnecessarily challenging to estimate from SVI
  3. It is more beneficial to combine both SVI and Non-SVI indicators

Challenges

  1. Quality of data, including datasets not large enough and survey bias
  2. The perspective of GSV SVI may influence the results, however, cycling paths are not prevalent in both selected areas.
  3. Large time and resources used in training and labeling.

Future Directions

  1. Incorporate predictive modelling to estimate AQI for each sample point
  2. Survey participants control
  3. Developing GUI software and API

Breakthroughs and Research Gaps

Apply New Models

In this study, the models used in feature extracting and labeling is time and resource consuming. One of our main aims is to apply new models in our research, including:

  • Zero-shot data labeling tool SAM

Connection with BLOS

A study by Khasharyar K reported that a significant issue in BLOS researches is that there is no consistent terminology in the field, and different research areas have been simultaneously work toward the aspects of cyclist confort that were not noticed nor considered in BLOS researches, like this study. Another aim of our study is to connect the method of this study with the idea of BLOS and include more indexes.
